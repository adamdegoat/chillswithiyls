Drinking Water in the Wild — Made Simple

Clear water from a stream can still make you seriously sick. Here's what you need to know to stay safe and hydrated out there.

When Do You Actually Need to Worry?

If you're at a campground with a tap, drink freely — that water is tested and treated, just like at home. You don't need to do anything to it. The moment to start thinking carefully about water safety is when you're sourcing water from nature: rivers, streams, lakes, or collected rainwater.

Natural water sources carry bacteria, parasites, and viruses that you can't see, smell, or taste. A crystal-clear mountain stream can still carry something that will put you on your back for a week. The water might have animals upstream, human activity, or natural contaminants that you'd never know about by looking.

The Two Ways to Make Water Safe

Boiling is the oldest and most reliable method. Bring the water to a full rolling boil — big bubbles, not just steam — and keep it there for one minute. That one minute kills everything: bacteria, parasites, viruses, all of it. Let it cool before you drink. It tastes completely normal. The only downside is that you need a stove or fire and a pot, and you have to wait for it to cool.

Filtering is the modern option, and it's brilliant for beginners. A LifeStraw or a Sawyer Squeeze filter costs between fifteen and thirty dollars from any camping or outdoor store. You fill a bag or bottle with water from the source, squeeze or sip it through the filter, and drink clean water immediately. No boiling, no waiting, no fuss. The filters last for years and are small enough to fit in a pocket. If you're doing any kind of hiking or camping where water access is uncertain, carry one.

Clear water is not always safe water — treat it first, every time.

The Rule You Cannot Break

Never drink straight from a river or stream, no matter how clean it looks. This is the single most important rule in this guide. Clear water is not safe water. The things that make water dangerous are invisible. It takes just one unlucky sip to get a waterborne illness that can ruin your whole trip — and in remote areas, leave you in a genuinely bad situation.

It doesn't matter if locals drink from that stream, or if you've seen animals drinking from it, or if it's flowing fast over rocks and looks pristine. Always treat it first. Always.

Carry More Than You Think You Need

Dehydration sneaks up on you outdoors. You're moving around more than usual, it might be warm, and you're not near a tap. The standard advice is two litres per person per day for mild activity — but in the heat, or on a hike, you can easily need double that.

Before any outing, fill more bottles than you think you'll need. It's a small inconvenience to carry an extra litre. It's a big problem to run out. Treat finding water sources as a bonus, not a plan. Bring what you need from home, and let any wild source you treat be a backup.
