🌱 Beginner Guide

Grow Real Food — Even on a Balcony

You don't need a garden, a green thumb, or any experience. You need a pot, some dirt, and a sunny windowsill.

Start With Herbs, Not Vegetables

The biggest beginner mistake is starting with vegetables. Vegetables take months, need more space, and can be surprisingly fussy. Herbs are fast, forgiving, and genuinely useful in the kitchen. When you see something you grew yourself going into your food, that feeling is what keeps you going.

Start with herbs and get one good harvest under your belt before you think about anything bigger. Once you've kept a herb alive for a month, your confidence grows right alongside it.

The Starter Four

Basil is the one most people start with. It grows fast, smells incredible, and goes into so many dishes. It needs warmth and good sunlight — a sunny windowsill or balcony is perfect. Mint is almost impossible to kill and spreads eagerly. Give it its own pot, because it will take over whatever container it shares. Chives are very low maintenance, grow back every time you cut them, and add flavour to eggs, potatoes, and salads all year round.

Cherry tomatoes are the gateway vegetable. They produce fruit on a balcony with surprisingly little fuss. They need a bigger pot — at least 30 centimetres across — and they'll need a stake or something to lean against as they grow. But the payoff is real, edible tomatoes from your own balcony, and that never stops being satisfying.

Herbs on a windowsill or balcony — the easiest place to start.

What You Actually Need

You need a pot with drainage holes in the bottom. This is not negotiable. Without drainage, water sits at the roots and the plant rots. Any pot from a hardware or garden store will have them. Fill it with potting mix — not garden soil dug from outside, not sand, not regular dirt. Potting mix from a bag is light, airy, and designed to hold just enough moisture while letting excess drain away.

You need a sunny spot. Most edible plants need at least four to six hours of direct sunlight per day. A north-facing balcony or a bright windowsill is ideal. If your spot is shady, stick to mint and chives — they'll cope. Basil and tomatoes need real sun.

Watering — the Finger Test

Overwatering kills more plants than anything else. People feel like they should water every day, so they do, and the roots drown. Instead, push your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels damp, leave it alone. If it feels dry, give it a good drink — enough that water drips from the drainage holes. Then leave it again until the finger test tells you otherwise.

In hot weather, you might be watering every day. In cool weather, every few days. Let the soil tell you, not the calendar.

A few pots, good soil, and a sunny spot — that's the whole setup.

Harvest Little and Often

Don't wait until the plant looks huge before you pick anything. Harvesting little and often actually encourages the plant to grow more. With basil, pinch off the top leaves regularly and it keeps branching out. With chives, cut what you need with scissors and they grow straight back. The more you harvest gently and regularly, the more food you get. It rewards the patient and the curious.
