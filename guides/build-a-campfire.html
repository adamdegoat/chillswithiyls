Build a Campfire That Actually Works

The reason most campfires fail is simple — people skip the first step. Here's how to get it right every time.

Three Types of Material, in Order

Every successful fire uses three layers of material, added in a specific order. Tinder is the first layer — this is the stuff that catches a spark immediately. Think dry leaves, crumpled newspaper, dried grass, or even a cotton ball. It needs to be bone dry and very fine. If tinder won't light, nothing else will.

Kindling is the second layer. These are small sticks, roughly pencil-sized. They catch from the burning tinder and hold the flame long enough to ignite the bigger wood. Snap them off dead branches — if they bend instead of snap, they're too wet.

Three layers — tinder, kindling, fuel. In that order, always.

Fuel is the third layer. These are proper logs, the size of your forearm. They're what actually keeps the fire going for hours. But you cannot jump straight to fuel. Fuel needs a strong, established flame to catch, and you only get that after the tinder and kindling have done their job.

Build It Like a Pyramid

Put your tinder in a loose pile in the centre of your fire pit. Lean your kindling sticks over the top in a pyramid shape — not a pile, a pyramid. Leave gaps between the sticks. Fire needs air just as much as it needs fuel. A tightly packed fire smothers itself.

Keep your fuel logs nearby but don't add them yet. You'll add them once the kindling is properly burning.

Light From the Bottom

Hold your lighter or match at the base of the tinder, not the top. Heat rises, so lighting from underneath means the flame travels upward through the material naturally. Give it a moment. If it goes out, blow gently at the base — not hard, just a soft, steady breath. You're feeding it oxygen.

One lighter beats matches every time in the outdoors. Wind kills matches. A cheap camping lighter is one of the best tools you can carry.

Feed It Small Before Big

Once the kindling is catching properly, add more kindling before you add any logs. This is where patience really pays off. A lot of people see a small flame and immediately pile on a big log, and the fire dies. Wait until you have a good bed of orange embers and a strong flame. Then add your first log. Add the second log a few minutes later. Let each piece establish before adding the next.

A well-built fire will last hours on just a few logs once it's going properly.

Patience at this stage is what separates a good fire from a frustrating one.

Always Put It Out Properly

Never leave a fire unattended, and never leave it smouldering when you go to sleep or leave the site. Pour water over it — more than you think you need. Then stir the ash and embers with a stick and pour more water. Keep going until there's no hissing and you can hold your hand over the ash without feeling any heat. That's a properly extinguished fire.
