🚗 Beginner Guide

Take Your Car Off-Road for the First Time

Most cars can handle a dirt track. The trick is knowing how to drive it — slow, steady, and thinking a few metres ahead.

Most Cars Can Do Light Off-Road

You don't need a lifted four-wheel-drive with a snorkel and a winch to go off-road. Most regular cars — even a small hatchback — can handle a graded dirt road just fine. The key is understanding the difference between a maintained dirt track (totally fine for any car) and actual rough terrain with deep ruts, mud, or creek crossings (that's where you need the right vehicle).

For your first time, stick to graded dirt roads and designated tracks. Leave the serious stuff for later, once you know what your car can handle.

Check Your Tyre Pressure First

On dirt and loose gravel, lower tyre pressure gives you more grip. It sounds strange, but a slightly softer tyre spreads out on the surface instead of sitting on top of it. Before you head onto a dirt track, let a little air out — aim for around 28 to 32 PSI instead of your normal on-road pressure. You'll feel the difference immediately. The car sits more confidently and steers more predictably.

Just remember to pump your tyres back up before you return to the highway. A tyre that's too soft at speed is a problem you don't want.

Go Slow and Look Ahead

Off-road driving is slow driving. Not creeping-at-a-snail's-pace slow, but calm and deliberate. The reason is simple: you need to see what's coming and choose your line before you get there. A rock, a rut, a soft patch — you handle all of these better when you spot them early and adjust your path, rather than reacting at the last second.

Keep your speed to a pace where you feel in control. If something doesn't look right ahead, stop, get out, and look at it on foot. That's not a sign of weakness. That's how experienced off-road drivers stay out of trouble.

Don't Spin Your Wheels

If you get stuck — on sand, mud, or loose dirt — your first instinct will be to accelerate harder. Don't. Spinning your wheels just digs you in deeper and makes the hole bigger. Instead, ease off the throttle completely, let the wheels stop spinning, and then try again very gently. Sometimes rocking the car slowly back and forth helps. Patience gets you out. Panic digs you in.

What 4WD Actually Means

In a normal car, power goes to either the front or rear wheels only. In a 4WD, power goes to all four wheels at once. That means four times the grip when you're on a slippery surface. Most modern SUVs have either full-time 4WD or an "auto" mode that engages automatically when it detects slip. If you have a dedicated 4WD mode, engage it before you need it — not after you're already stuck.

Start With a Designated Track

Find a marked 4WD track or a campground with a dirt access road. These are graded and maintained for exactly this purpose. Don't just drive off into random bush — you won't know what the ground is like, and getting stuck somewhere remote on your first outing is no fun. Designated tracks are where you learn, build confidence, and have a great time without the risk.
