Red Ground, Black Sky: The Gear That Held When the Plateau Tried to Take Everything

A calm afternoon on a South Australian plateau turns violent in 40 minutes — and a converted Sprinter becomes the only thing between one traveller and an indifferent sky.

The wind doesn't know about your awning arm.

Forty metres of shadow sliding across ochre rock is the first thing you notice. Not cloud shadow — this is denser, more purposeful, the kind that moves with intention across a landscape that usually has none. I was standing outside the van on the Arckaringa plateau, watching the light change from flat white to something amber and wrong, and I remember thinking: that's fast. That's the moment the day becomes a different kind of day.

The plateau had been cooperative since I'd driven up the escarpment track three days earlier — the converted Mercedes Sprinter riding the corrugations with the confidence of good light-truck all-terrains and a properly weighted build. Late May in the South Australian outback. Days around 22°C (72°F), nights dropping to eight (mid-40s°F), sky a ceramic blue that makes you feel you're living inside a piece of good pottery. I had parked on a flat shelf of Cretaceous sandstone, a little below the true plateau edge, with a 270-degree view of a valley filled with mulga scrub and the kind of silence that has actual weight. Good camp. Thought I'd read the terrain well.

I had not read it well enough.

The question that drives every desert camping decision — and the one I failed to fully answer on that first afternoon — is this: where does the wind come from, and what will it find when it gets here?

On a closed forest track or a rocky gorge, a vehicle provides its own shelter. On a red desert plateau, there is nothing. No tree line, no ridge break, nothing between you and a 400-kilometre fetch of open, heated country to the northwest. The plateau amplifies everything. When a front moves through, it doesn't arrive gently. It arrives as a wall.

I knew this, in the abstract way you know things before they test you. What I had done wrong was park for the view rather than the wind. Backed the van in facing south — great for the evening light, irrelevant when what matters is your broadside exposure to the northwest. The van was sitting with its full 6.2-metre (20 ft) flank presented to the direction the weather would come from. When that shadow reached me and I checked the Bureau of Meteorology radar on the Garmin inReach, I saw a long orange crescent of a storm cell moving southeast at roughly 55 km/h (34 mph). I had maybe 40 minutes.

This is the part I'd rather not tell you, but it's the only part worth telling.

My 270-degree awning — a quality Australian-made unit I'd had for two years — was out. Fully deployed. Three and a half metres (11.5 ft) of canopy, extended and tensioned with the side panel down, facing exactly the wrong direction. I had set it up that morning for shade. It was now an enormous, beautifully constructed sail.

The right answer, at 40 minutes out, is to drop the awning immediately and deal with nothing else until that's done. Collapse it, roll it, lock the arms. Accept that you might lose the camp comfort. The wrong answer is what I did: I decided I had time to drop the side panel, reposition a guy line, and try to partially tension the awning into the wind. I was thinking about the hour it would take to redeploy once the storm passed. I was thinking about coffee. I was not thinking about the fact that a gusted leading edge on a front like that can arrive 15 minutes before your radar estimate, at speeds the radar can't quite capture.

At the 20-minute mark, the wall of dust hit.

Dust storms in the Australian interior are not the photogenic orange walls you see in pictures, though this one had that quality at distance. Up close, at the moment of impact, they are brown noise and grit and a sustained roar that you feel in your sinuses. The temperature dropped four degrees in about as many minutes. And the wind — the actual sustained wind, not the gusts — came in at what I later estimated from the van's weather station was close to 65 km/h (40 mph), with gusts peaking somewhere north of 80 (50 mph).

The awning arm went at the second gust. Not catastrophically — the plastic end cap shattered and the arm buckled at the telescoping section, but the fabric held because I'd had the presence of mind to run a guy line to a ground anchor before the front hit. That guy line probably saved the awning from going entirely. But the arm was bent beyond field repair, and the canopy was now flogging against the side of the van with a sound like repeated rifle shots. I got it down in about eight minutes, hands grey with grit, eyes streaming. An eight-minute lesson in the cost of optimism.

What I want to talk about here is ground anchors, because they are the most undervalued piece of shelter hardware in desert overlanding, and because on this trip they earned every gram I'd carried.

On rock — and Cretaceous plateau sandstone is surprisingly variable, from solid slabs to fractured scree — standard sand pegs are useless. They skate across the surface or bite into nothing. What works is a combination of approaches. I carry a set of 230mm (9 in) screw-in auger anchors designed for sandy soil, which have adequate holding on compacted red earth between rock outcrops. But the real anchor system on this trip was a set of three 8mm natural webbing loops threaded under rock slabs, weighted with additional stones and backed with a taut line running to the awning's guy points at a 45-degree angle. The physics of this is simple: a line running at 45 degrees from the top of an anchor point distributes the load into both horizontal and vertical vectors, which means you're fighting the wind load and the lift simultaneously. A vertical anchor only fights lift. On a plateau with no peg purchase, you need both.

The guy lines I use are 5mm dyneema — not paracord, not the cheap elastic bungee lines that come with most awnings. Dyneema has almost no stretch under load, which means the anchor system is doing its job from the moment the load arrives, not after 30 centimetres of elastic travel. Under sustained 65 km/h wind, elastic lines fail because they allow the structure to oscillate, and oscillation is what destroys awning arms and fabric. Rigid tension wins. The one guy line I had properly rigged before the front hit held the awning arm in place for the full duration of the gust. The other arms, which I had not yet retensioned, buckled.

Verdict: in desert plateau conditions, deploy ground anchors before you need them, rig guy lines at 45 degrees, and use low-stretch cordage. The awning system isn't primarily about the awning; it's about the anchor system, and the anchor system needs to suit the ground you're actually on, not the ground you wish you were on.

With the awning down and stowed — or rather, rolled and bungeed to the roof rack in a state I'd deal with later — I had about 15 minutes of margin before the dust storm became something else. The radar showed rain behind the dust: a squall line that would drop the temperature further and add horizontal rain to whatever the wind was already doing.

This is where the van itself becomes the critical shelter decision.

The Sprinter has been converted over three years, with a genuine eye on four-season capability. High roof. Composite insulated wall panels with 75mm (3 in) of closed-cell foam. Diesel heater plumbed under the passenger seat, fed by a 40-litre (10.5 gal) tank that's separate from the driving fuel. 200Ah of lithium iron phosphate batteries fed by a 300W rooftop solar array plus a DC-to-DC charger on the alternator. It is, in the language of expedition building, a serious platform. But a serious platform still has vulnerabilities, and a plateau storm at dusk reveals all of them.

The first decision was whether to move. This is the one I want to dwell on, because I've thought about it since. The plateau track out — down the escarpment — was already in questionable condition before the rain. The red earth up here is the kind of clay-rich ground that looks solid and is solid when dry, but 20 minutes of moderate rain turns into something close to wet soap, with zero lateral traction. I have seen fully articulated, diff-locked 80 Series Land Cruisers lose it on this stuff. Moving in a front-wheel-drive-based van — even with all the modifications — on a wet clay escarpment in near-zero visibility was not a sane option.

So you stay. And staying means the van is your only shelter, and you need to make it work.

The orientation question — which I couldn't fully correct given the storm's arrival — matters enormously for two reasons. First, aerodynamic load. A van sitting broadside to 80 km/h wind is experiencing significant lateral force; my build sits high and the roof rack adds windage. I repositioned as much as I could: turned the van approximately 30 degrees to present more of the nose to the northwest rather than the flank. Not ideal, but it reduced the broadside exposure meaningfully. Even that partial correction reduced the van's rocking motion by a noticeable degree. Second, windows. Every window on the van's broadside was taking direct impact. The driver and passenger windows are glass. The living area windows are double-glazed acrylic in aluminium frames, which are far better, but the rear barn doors have glass and relatively thin seals.

I had made external window covers out of 6mm correx board and rubber-edged aluminium channel the previous year — cut to size, stored flat under the bed platform. In the 10 minutes I had before the rain arrived, I got three of them up: the two large side living-area windows and the offside rear. The covers clip to the existing window frame lips with modified cam-lock clasps. They do two things: they block wind-driven grit from finding the window seals, and they add a meaningful layer of insulation. The U-value of a single acrylic window in a cold wind is poor regardless of double glazing; a correx cover adds dead air and cuts the convective loss significantly.

The fourth cover — the nearside rear — I did not get up in time. I paid for this later with a window frame seal that leaked a fine mist of rain-driven grit for three hours. Not dangerous, just aggravating, and a lesson in sequencing: the windows facing the weather direction go up first, not last.

Ventilation was the next call, and it's a harder one than it sounds. The instinct in a cold storm is to seal everything. The problem is moisture. One person breathing, cooking and running a heater generates significant condensation in a sealed vehicle. My rooftop Maxxair fan has a motorised lid that seals from inside; I ran it on the lowest setting in extraction mode, just enough to maintain a slight negative pressure relative to outside. This pulls air out rather than in, which means any infiltration through imperfect seals is outward rather than inward, and you're managing condensation rather than accumulating it. In a lateral rain situation, the roof vent is protected by its own rain dome; it leaked nothing.

The diesel heater came on at around 6pm, with the outside temperature by then sitting at 3°C (37°F) and falling. I want to give this piece of kit the verdict it deserves: a properly installed, properly fuelled diesel heater in a well-insulated van is categorically superior to any other off-grid heating solution for an expedition-style vehicle. It burns approximately 0.1–0.15 litres of diesel per hour at moderate output — at that rate, my 40-litre tank gives me weeks of overnight heating. It produces no meaningful interior condensation, because combustion air is drawn from outside and exhaust is vented outside. It runs entirely on 12V from the lithium bank, drawing perhaps 10W once the glow-plug cycle is done. On a night that dropped to -2°C (28°F) outside, I had the van interior sitting at 17°C (63°F) with the heater running at roughly 40% output. The battery bank dropped 18Ah overnight between the heater, the LED lighting and a phone charge. By 9am the following morning, with weak winter sun, the solar had replaced 35Ah. This is what energy self-sufficiency looks like in practice: not infinite power, but a closed loop that works across a 24-hour cycle in most conditions.

The storm ran its course in about six hours. By 11pm the wind had dropped to a manageable 25 km/h (16 mph), the rain had stopped, and the plateau had turned genuinely cold under clearing skies. I went outside at midnight to check the anchors I'd left in place on the van's wheel positions — small flat-blade sand anchors looped to the recovery points, which I'd deployed as a precaution against any tendency for the van to shift on the wet clay. Unnecessary as it turned out — the van's weight and the low centre of gravity from the lithium and water tanks under the floor made it utterly stable. But the habit of anchoring is a good one. On a different surface, in a longer event, it would matter.

What I found outside was extraordinary: a plateau that had been orange and dusty and warm 12 hours earlier was now iron-grey under a near-full moon, with a skin of snow on the high ground to the east and the red earth turned black with moisture. The temperature was sitting at -2°C. The van's roof had a patina of frozen grit. Everything smelled of wet rock and something faintly metallic — the iron in the soil, liberated by the rain.

I stayed out for about 20 minutes. Long enough to understand that the plateau had not been hostile. It had been indifferent, which is something else entirely, and more honest. The wind doesn't know about your awning arm.

The next morning I did a full damage assessment. Awning arm: buckled, replaceable. Rear window seal: grit infiltration, cleanable. One guy line anchor: pulled from the ground during the peak gust, meaning the webbing loop under the rock slab had lost friction as the clay softened in the rain. I had not anticipated that the anchor medium — clay-packed rock crevice — would change its holding properties mid-event as the storm evolved from dust to rain. This is worth knowing: in mixed-event conditions, rock-weighted webbing anchors can lose significant holding power as surface clay becomes wet and lubricating. The screw-in auger anchors set into deep, compacted earth held without issue; the rock-weighted surface anchors did not. The lesson here is to reserve your most critical guy points for screw-in or mechanical anchors, and use rock weighting only as a secondary or supplemental load path.

Power was fine throughout. The lithium bank handled the overnight load, the solar recovered it the next day, the diesel heater ran uninterrupted. This is the central point of the whole off-grid argument: if you've done the work on the energy system, it becomes invisible in an event. You're not thinking about batteries during a storm; you're thinking about the storm. The platform should disappear.

There are a few real rules that came out of that night, and I want to state them plainly.

First: on any plateau or open exposed terrain, orient your camp for wind before you orient it for view. Walk the site. Feel the dominant wind direction. Check the forecast before you park, not after. The view will still be there if you're 15 metres east of where you'd have parked for the shot.

Second: when a weather event is incoming, drop the awning. The whole awning. Not reorganise it, not try to retension it. Get it down, get it stowed, and do it first, before any other camp prep. An awning arm costs several hundred dollars and weeks of lead time. Ten minutes of inconvenience costs nothing.

Third: your anchor system should match your actual substrate, not your assumed substrate, and it should account for how that substrate changes when wet. This is genuinely overlooked. The same patch of desert ground that holds a screw-in anchor at 150kg (330 lb) pull-out strength when dry may hold it at 60–70% of that when saturated. Design to the wet-ground figure, not the dry-ground figure.

Fourth: van orientation relative to wind direction is a variable you control, and it's worth the two minutes it takes to correct it. Presenting the nose rather than the broadside reduces both aerodynamic load and the psychological unpleasantness of a van rocking on its suspension for six hours. The aero-load reduction is real: a typical high-roof van turned 30 degrees off broadside sheds a meaningful fraction of the lateral force it would otherwise carry. It's not a calculation you do in the field, but understanding the principle is enough.

And the one rule I would carry off a plateau and hand to anyone heading into serious exposed terrain: the gear that matters is the gear you've already tested in conditions that weren't trying to kill you. Not unpacked from the box at the moment you need it, not assembled by torchlight in 65 km/h wind. Your awning anchor system, your window covers, your sealing routine — these need to be so practised that they become motor memory. Because when the shadow moves across the red rock and you've got 40 minutes, you don't have time to think. You have time to do.

The awning arm got welded in Marree two days later. The van climbed the escarpment when the clay dried, which took most of a day. I spent that day reading, brewing tea, watching a wedge-tailed eagle work a thermal above the escarpment edge. The plateau was indifferent to this too. That's the deal.
