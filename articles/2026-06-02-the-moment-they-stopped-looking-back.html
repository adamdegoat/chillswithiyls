The Moment They Stopped Looking Back

A Kuantan family's 260-litre water system — atmospheric generation, fog mesh, and staged filtration — that lets them camp fourteen days on a Terengganu dune with no resupply.

The alternator charges the batteries that run the GENNY that makes the water that lets you stay.

The first thing Priya Subramaniam noticed, three hours after they'd aired down and driven the Hilux onto the sand shelf above Pantai Kemasik, was that she couldn't hear the road anymore. Not just the sound of it — the idea of it. The tarmac anxiety, the fuel math, the exit strategy. It was all still there in theory, but theory had stopped mattering somewhere around the second kilometre of soft sand when concentration took over and left no room for anything else. Her husband Dev was focused on his line, reading the camber, keeping momentum without overcooking the throttle. Their two kids were watching the sea through the rear windows like they'd never seen it before, which in a sense they hadn't — not from here, not at this angle, not with a hundred kilometres of nothing scheduled after dinner.

She looked at the fuel gauge. She looked at the 240-litre water system mounted behind the rear seats in a modified tub-and-tank configuration that Dev had been refining for three years. She did not look back.

That was eighteen months ago. Since then, the Subramaniam family has run nine extended coastal dune camps along the Terengganu and Pahang shoreline, the longest a fourteen-day stay at a site near Kuala Abang where the only fresh water within forty kilometres was what they generated themselves. This is the story of how they built a system capable of doing that — and the one afternoon in the middle of camp four when it nearly unravelled.

Water is the constraint that ends coastal camping for most people before it begins. The assumption is that the coast is wet — the sea is right there, it rains regularly, the air is thick with moisture. All of that is true and none of it helps. Saltwater is undrinkable without equipment that runs on serious power and generates brine waste. Rain on a coastal dune site drains through the sand so fast that by the time you've set up a collection surface, most of what fell is already three metres down and mixing with the brackish lens beneath the dune system. Dig a hole anywhere near the tide line and you'll hit water within ninety centimetres, but it tastes like a saltlick and will dehydrate you faster than it rehydrates you. The freshwater table in active dune terrain is either very deep, very contaminated, or both.

"Everyone thinks coastal equals easy," Dev said, sitting outside the Hilux in a camp chair at Pantai Cherating on a morning when the humidity was sitting around eighty-three percent. "The water table is right there. The air is basically water. And you're still sitting here working for every drop."

Working for it is exactly what they do — and the methodology they developed over three years of iteration is a combination of atmospheric water generation, passive fog collection, and staged pressure filtration, all of it integrated into the Hilux platform in a way that runs almost entirely on what the vehicle can generate from its own power systems.

Dev Subramaniam is a mechanical engineer from Kuantan who spent a decade working on industrial cooling systems before pivoting to a smaller consultancy doing building services. He is not a YouTube overlander. He does not have a merchandise line. His Hilux — a 2018 Revo double cab in grey that has been progressively modified over four years — does not have a roof tent or a light bar. It has a snorkel, an ARB air locker on the rear axle, BFGoodrich KO2s in 265/70R17, and a traction board set that lives in a slot-mounted bracket on the driver's side. None of that is what makes it interesting.

What makes it interesting is what happens between the B-pillars and the tailgate.

The rear tray has been fitted with a custom aluminium canopy fabricated by a small shop in Kuantan that Dev has been working with since his first build. The canopy runs the full tray length and adds 180 millimetres of internal headroom over the tray rails. Inside that space, along the driver's side wall, sits the primary water storage: two 120-litre polyethylene tanks, the kind rated for food contact, mounted on a sleeved rail system that allows them to be pulled out individually for cleaning. Combined with a twenty-litre day-use bladder mounted in the rear cabin footwell, total carrying capacity is two hundred and sixty litres. At two litres per person per day for drinking and cooking — they camp simply, no showers — that's a theoretical thirty-two days of water for a family of four before any generation kicks in.

But they don't carry two hundred and sixty litres out of the city. They carry eighty. The rest is made on site.

The generation system has three components, and understanding why each exists requires understanding the specific conditions of a Terengganu dune camp.

The first is an atmospheric water generator: a Watergen GENNY unit that Dev acquired secondhand from a building developer who'd used it for a construction site office. It's a condensation-cycle machine — air is drawn in, cooled below the dew point, water is collected and passes through a carbon and UV filtration stage. The GENNY is rated to produce between 10 and 30 litres per day depending on ambient temperature and humidity; in the coastal conditions they operate in, where overnight humidity rarely drops below seventy-five percent, they reliably get between 22 and 28 litres per day. The unit draws roughly 1.5 kW to run. That is the whole problem.

"The machine works perfectly," Dev said. "Powering it is the engineering project."

The Hilux's auxiliary power system is built around a 200Ah lithium iron phosphate battery bank — two 100Ah Battle Born batteries in parallel — charged by a 300-watt folding solar array that deploys from a bracket on the canopy roof. In the coastal tropics, useful solar hours are roughly five to six per day, yielding 1.5 to 1.8 kilowatt-hours of solar energy daily. Running the GENNY for six hours at night — when humidity peaks and power demand for everything else drops — consumes about 9 kWh. More than the panel can generate in a day.

The gap is closed by running the Hilux engine for ninety minutes each morning, using a Victron MultiPlus inverter-charger to harvest power from a DC-DC charger connected to the vehicle's alternator. The Revo's alternator puts out 130 amps at 14.4 volts; through the DC-DC charger, they recover around 50 amps usefully into the lithium bank, which at ninety minutes is roughly 90–95Ah, or about 1.3 kWh. Combined with the solar yield, they're generating 2.8 to 3.1 kWh per day, which powers six to eight hours of GENNY operation overnight plus lighting, a fan, and phone charging. The fuel cost for ninety minutes of idle-to-light-load running is approximately 1.5 litres of diesel per day — a cost they consider the price of water independence.

The second component is a passive fog collection system, which Dev is more proud of than any of the powered equipment. It came out of a conversation with a Malaysian permaculture practitioner he'd met at an overlanding event in Bentong, who pointed him toward research done in the Canary Islands and the Atacama Desert on fog nets. A fog collection mesh — a polypropylene shade cloth with around forty percent void ratio, mounted vertically and perpendicular to the prevailing onshore breeze — can passively collect water from coastal fog and sea spray. The droplets impact the mesh fibres, coalesce, and run down to a collection trough at the bottom.

Their mesh is six metres wide by two metres tall — twelve square metres total — made from sixty-percent shade cloth purchased from an agricultural supplier in Kemaman. It mounts on a pair of fibreglass tent poles with guy lines and deploys in about fifteen minutes. On heavy fog mornings, which happen perhaps three or four times per week on the Terengganu coast between October and February, it yields between four and nine litres per night. On clear dry nights, almost nothing. It's not a primary system. It's a supplement that cost less than RM200 to build and requires nothing to run.

"You don't design around what it gives you," Dev said. "You just never say no to free water."

The third component is filtration and treatment. All water — from the GENNY, from the fog mesh, from any emergency rainwater collection — passes through a three-stage inline filter setup mounted in the canopy: a 5-micron sediment pre-filter, a 0.1-micron hollow fibre membrane filter (a Sawyer Squeeze element in a pressure-capable housing that Dev machined himself), and a final UV-C treatment stage using a SteriPen Adventurer running in a small clear tube. The whole thing is driven by a twelve-volt Shurflo pressure pump. Filtered water comes out of a stainless spigot on the canopy's passenger-side wall.

The output pressure is around 30 PSI. It fills a one-litre bottle in about forty-five seconds. It tastes like water.

Camp four was at a site south of Dungun, a broad sand shelf backed by casuarina trees that a friend had scouted and recommended. They arrived on a Thursday with enough stored water for about twelve days and plans to stay for ten. By Saturday morning, the GENNY had stopped producing.

Not stopped running — it ran fine. The compressor cycled, the fan turned, the indicator lights did what they always did. But the output had dropped from the usual 22 litres overnight to less than three litres, and the water had a faint but distinct metallic smell that Priya noticed when she filled the kettle.

Dev spent two hours pulling the unit apart in the heat of mid-morning. What he found was that the internal heat exchanger — the component that chills the air below dew point — had developed a hairline crack in a brazed joint, likely from repeated vibration over corrugated roads leading to the coast. The crack allowed a small amount of refrigerant to contaminate the condensate. The machine was producing water, but the water was not clean.

"I knew exactly what it was the moment I smelt it," he said. "I just didn't want to know."

They had two days of stored water remaining in the day bladder. The primary tanks had been drawn down over the previous two days. The fog mesh had given them almost nothing — the skies had been clear and there was no onshore fog. Their nearest resupply was a petrol station forty-two kilometres away on a road they'd have to partially retrace over coastal track before hitting tarmac.

They left camp that morning, resupplied, and drove back. Round trip took five and a half hours. They spent that evening reviewing what had gone wrong.

The lessons were specific. First: the GENNY lives in the tray canopy, which is the most vibration-exposed location on the vehicle. It needs vibration-dampening mounts — not the rubber foot pads that came stock. Dev now runs it on a set of Sorbothane isolation mounts that cut transmitted vibration by around sixty percent. Second: never let stored water drop below a four-day buffer before checking generation output, not just the running state of the machine. The GENNY had been giving degraded output for probably twelve hours before anyone noticed. Third: carry a basic refrigerant leak detector. It's a small thing, available at any air-conditioning supply shop, but it would have caught the problem the first morning.

The failure didn't end the trip. They went back and finished the ten days. But it reconfigured how Dev thinks about system redundancy.

"Every component has a backup now except the GENNY, because the GENNY is too heavy to double. So the fog mesh is the backup. The backup for the fog mesh is carrying more initial water. And the backup for that is knowing exactly how far the resupply run is from wherever you're going before you leave."

He now draws a simple diagram for any new camp site: the water radius. It shows the camp location, the nearest resupply point, the distance and road type, and the minimum buffer he needs to maintain before that run becomes necessary. It takes five minutes to make and has changed how he plans every trip since camp four.

The Hilux itself deserves more than a footnote in this story. The water system exists because the Hilux can get to places where the system needs to operate — and the choice of what to modify on the vehicle reflects the same logic as the water system: function over form, nothing that doesn't earn its weight.

The rear air locker on the ARB unit was the first modification, fitted after Dev beached the truck on a soft sand camber at Pantai Balok on their second trip and spent forty minutes recovering it alone while the kids built something in the sand. "I was sitting there thinking, one wheel spinning, three wheels doing nothing, this is embarrassing." The locker eliminated that failure mode entirely.

The KO2s are aired down to 16 PSI on soft sand, 22 PSI on coastal packed gravel, 35 PSI on tarmac. Dev travels with a 12V ARB twin compressor mounted under the bonnet for re-inflation; the time to air back up from 16 to 35 PSI across all four tyres is about fourteen minutes, which is exactly how long it takes to pack up the fog mesh and stow the camp chairs. It was not designed to work out that way, but Dev is pleased that it does.

The extended-range fuel setup — a 40-litre auxiliary tank fitted in the passenger-side tray drawer module — extends the effective range to around 900 kilometres, which in practice means the Subramaniam family has never spent cognitive energy worrying about fuel during a coastal dune camp. Water is the constraint. Fuel is not. That deliberate rebalancing of constraints is part of the design philosophy.

Priya manages the food and power logistics, and she describes the system not in engineering terms but in days. "I think in days. We have food for this many days. Water for this many days. Power generation. Fuel. If all those numbers say the same number, we're ready." The longest number currently in their system — the fourteen-day limit set by tank capacity and generation rate in good conditions — defines the outer edge of what's possible on any single run.

They have not yet hit that limit in a single camp. Their longest has been eleven days, at Kuala Abang. Priya said it felt like a different kind of time. Not longer — different. Like the unit of measurement changed.

Their daughter Kavya is twelve and has strong opinions about camp food, which she prepares most nights on a single-burner MSR Windburner. Their son Aryan is nine and is developing a serious interest in the fog mesh, which he monitors each morning and records in a notebook with dates, weather observations, and yield figures. He presented his data at school and his teacher asked if he'd made it up. He had not.

These details are not incidental. The Subramaniam family runs extended coastal dune camps because the family is the point. The technical system is what makes it logistically possible for a family of four to be genuinely self-sufficient in a high-difficulty environment without a support vehicle, without a guide, and without a return date controlled by a water bottle count. That is what the system buys — not the experience of camping, but the quality of presence that becomes available when the support logistics fade into the background and stop demanding attention.

"The first few trips, I was thinking about the system constantly," Dev said. "Is it running? What's the level? What's tomorrow looking like for the mesh?" He paused. "Now I check it twice a day, like a gauge. And then I stop thinking about it."

That is the actual design goal. A system you stop thinking about.

There are things they would change and things they would not touch.

The GENNY stays, with the Sorbothane mounts. The fog mesh stays in exactly its current form — twelve square metres of agricultural shade cloth and two fibreglass poles — because it is simple enough to never fail in a way that matters. The Sawyer membrane filter housing stays, because in three years of heavy use the membrane has not required replacement, which is a data point worth respecting.

What Dev would change is the solar array. Three hundred watts of foldable panel is adequate, but only just. He wants to add a roof-mounted rigid panel of 200 to 250 watts on a tiltable frame to supplement the foldable array, which would push daily solar yield close to 4 kWh and allow the engine run to be reduced from ninety minutes to forty-five. That halves the fuel cost and the noise. The weight penalty is around 18 kilograms on the canopy roof, which he has modelled and considers acceptable on the KO2s. He has not yet done it because the fabrication shop in Kuantan is backed up and he is not in a hurry.

What Priya would change is the day bladder location. A twenty-litre bladder in the rear footwell sounds practical until one of your children steps on the fill cap at 11pm and soaks the sleeping bag storage area. It is moving to the canopy interior on the next revision, in a purpose-built bracket, with a lockable cap.

What they would both not change under any circumstances is the decision to stop looking at the rear-view mirror for roads. That is not a modification. It is the point of all the modifications.

If you are planning a coastal dune camp in similar terrain — Terengganu, Pahang, the Sigatoka dunes on Fiji's Coral Coast, the dunefields above Lancelin in Western Australia — the water problem will find you in some form. The Subramaniam system is not the only way to solve it, but the principles behind it apply everywhere: know your consumption rate, know your generation rate, know the distance and road condition to your nearest resupply, and never let your buffer compress below the resupply time. Build the fog mesh even if you think the conditions don't warrant it. The material cost is negligible and the nights that surprise you with fog will become mornings you feel pleased about.

The GENNY needs to be on isolated mounts if it lives in a vehicle tray. That is not a suggestion — it's what the crack in the brazed joint at camp four is telling you. Refrigerant contamination is slow and subtle enough that you'll drink the water before you notice something's wrong if you're not checking carefully. A small refrigerant leak detector, the kind used for home air-conditioning service, fits in a glasses case and gives you an answer in ten seconds.

And the Hilux — or whatever 4x4 you run — is not separate from the water system. It is part of it. The alternator charges the batteries that run the GENNY that makes the water that lets you stay. The air locker gets you onto the site where the fog mesh can face the onshore breeze. These things are continuous with each other. Build them that way.

Aryan Subramaniam's notebook currently has data from eleven camp sites over eighteen months. The highest single-night fog mesh yield recorded is 11.4 litres, at Pantai Kemasik, on a night when the wind shifted onshore at about 2am and the mesh was facing directly into it by luck. He has noted this and written the word "ANGLE" in capitals next to the entry, with an arrow and a question mark.

He is nine years old and already asking the right questions.
