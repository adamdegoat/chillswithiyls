REDARC REDWorks Power Panel

Pre-wired, plug-and-play off-grid power

REDARC's REDWorks Power Panel is a purpose-built, pre-wired electrical management unit that integrates solar input, DC-DC charging, and load distribution into a single panel — designed to replace the rat's nest most DIY overlanders are running behind their drawer systems. Debuted at Overland Expo West 2026, it addresses the single most common failure point in overlanding electrical builds: poor wiring decisions made under pressure. One honest limitation: it is a REDARC-ecosystem product, meaning it pairs best with REDARC's own battery chargers and monitors — mixing brands introduces compatibility headaches.

~AUD $450–600 (approx. USD $280–380)
Trail Intel

Endau Rompin National Park — Selai Entry, Pahang, Malaysia

Unsealed 4WD track from Kahang; min 200mm ground clearance required, low range essential. Expect knee-deep water crossings in wet season.

Most overlanders approach Endau Rompin from the Peta gateway in Johor — there's a permit office, vehicle shuttles, and clear signage. The Selai entry in Pahang is a different proposition: 4WD-only, longer approach track, no shuttle service, and a road that becomes impassable without recovery gear from October through February. What you get in return is the entire northern section of one of the oldest rainforests on earth almost entirely to yourself. The park's interior hasn't changed since the last ice age — it shows.

Best season: March–September (dry season; hard avoid October–February)
Build Tip

Wiring a DC-DC charger to your auxiliary battery

  1. Size the charger to your alternator: a 40A charger on a 100A alternator is healthy; beyond 40% of rated alternator output you risk heat and long-term wear. When in doubt, go smaller.
  2. Run dedicated cable directly from the starter battery positive terminal to the charger input — minimum 6mm² (AWG 10) for a 20A charger, 10mm² (AWG 8) for a 40A. Never tap into an existing accessory circuit.
  3. Fuse within 300mm of each battery connection — one at the starter battery, one at the auxiliary. Fuse rating matches cable capacity, not charger draw.
  4. Mount the charger in a ventilated location: DC-DC units generate significant heat at full load and will throttle output if ambient temperature exceeds their thermal threshold. Under the bonnet is common; inside an enclosed drawer cabinet is not.
  5. Connect the sense wire (if present) to the starter battery positive so the charger reads actual voltage and avoids false termination — this alone can add 10–15% to your auxiliary charge capacity per drive.

Practical Knowledge

Tips for the Trail & the Mind

Field-tested camping know-how and the mindset shifts that make off-grid living stick.

⛺ Camping Tips

  • Set camp before you're tiredDecision-making degrades fast in the late afternoon after a long drive. If you're choosing a campsite while hungry and exhausted, you'll make poor decisions — camping on a slope, facing the wind wrong, picking a site that drains into your tent. Arrive early, or stop earlier than feels necessary.
  • Level your sleeping surface, not just your tentA tent that looks flat often has a subtle slope you won't notice until 3am, when you've slid to one end of your sleeping bag. Lie flat in the spot before you stake down. Add a folded towel under the pad if needed — your back will thank you by morning three.
  • Grey water goes 50 metres from waterWashing-up water — even with biodegradable soap — carries oils, food particles, and bacteria. Scatter it well away from rivers and streams, in a wide area on open ground where it can filter through soil. This rule matters more in hot climates where breakdown is slower than it looks.
  • Fire safety starts with the siteBefore you light anything, clear a 1.5-metre radius of dry vegetation and know where the wind is going. A fire that stays manageable is built in a cleared area on mineral soil — not a ring of rocks sitting on top of leaf litter, which insulates heat and can ignite the ground below you.
  • Peg all four corners before you sleepLeaving a corner unpegged 'just for tonight' is how tents end up across the campsite. Even calm nights surprise you, especially at altitude or in open terrain. Stake every corner, then run a finger along each seam. Do it once and you will do it forever.
  • Water is always heavier than you planned forStandard advice is 2L of water per person per day. In Southeast Asian heat, budget 4L minimum and carry 6L if you're not certain of a water source along the way. Dehydration in the tropics happens fast and recovers slow — by the time you're thirsty, you're already behind.

🌱 Self-Reliance & Mindset

  • Slow down before you're forced toMost people arrive at camp still running at city speed — urgent, task-focused, checking things off. If you don't consciously downshift, you'll spend the first half of your trip being efficient instead of present. The transition is a choice, not a feeling that arrives on its own.
  • Discomfort is information, not failureThe rain, the bad sleep, the meal that didn't work — none of it means you're doing it wrong. Discomfort is your system recalibrating to conditions without padding. The useful question is not 'how do I fix this' but 'what does this teach me about what I actually need.'
  • Leave marginBuild more time into a trip than you think you need. The buffer is where the best moments happen — the spontaneous swim, the campfire conversation that ran long, the unexpected track you decided to follow. Tight itineraries trade those moments for logistics.
  • Know your baseline before you leave itYou will not know what genuinely matters to you about daily life until you have removed it for a week. The things you miss are information. The things you forget to miss are more information. Don't come home and immediately rebuild the old routine without reading what the absence taught you.
  • The truck is not the pointThe vehicle, the gear, the setup — none of it matters as much as the decision to go. People with a bare-bones setup and a clear destination consistently have better trips than people with a perfect rig and no particular goal. Start with where you want to be. The gear follows.
  • Failure is load-bearingEvery experienced off-gridder or overlander you admire has a catalogue of things that went wrong. They are not successful despite those failures — they are capable because of them. The mistake is not a detour from the learning. It is the learning.
