Gear Pick

ARB CKMA12 Twin Motor Air Compressor

The on-board air solution that actually works

Permanently mounted under the bonnet, the CKMA12 runs on a 12V feed and delivers 72 litres per minute — fast enough to re-inflate a set of 35-inch mud terrains in under ten minutes per tyre. That means you can air down for soft terrain at the start of a track and be back to road pressure before you hit the highway without hunting for a servo. The honest limitation: installation requires routing a wiring loom through the engine bay and finding a solid mounting point — budget 3–4 hours the first time if you're unfamiliar with the engine bay.

$350–$450 USD
Trail Intel

Endau-Rompin National Park, Johor/Pahang, Malaysia

Laterite 4WD track from Kahang (Johor entry) or Kuala Rompin (Pahang entry); minimum 200mm ground clearance, high-clearance 4WD required in wet season

The permit system is the part most people underestimate — both the Johor National Parks Corporation and Pahang Forestry Department cap daily visitor numbers, and permits sell out weeks ahead during school holidays. Book the permit before you plan the trip, not after. The Kahang entry is more accessible for day trips; the Kuala Rompin side requires a longer commitment but delivers remote river camping the Johor approach can't match.

Best season: March–September (avoid northeast monsoon Nov–Jan; some tracks impassable)
Build Tip

Wiring a second battery with a B2B charger for campsite power

  1. Size your auxiliary battery to your actual load: 100Ah AGM for basic campsite lighting and phone charging; 100Ah lithium for running a 40L fridge for 2–3 days. Measure first, buy second.
  2. Install a Battery-to-Battery (B2B) charger between the starter and aux — not a simple voltage-sensitive relay (VSR). Modern smart alternators drop voltage in ways that confuse VSRs and leave the aux chronically undercharged. A B2B converts alternator output properly for AGM or lithium chemistry.
  3. Route the positive cable through an inline fuse positioned within 30cm of the starting battery — sized at 10–20% above your maximum expected draw. This fuse protects the vehicle, not the aux circuit.
  4. Ground both batteries to the chassis at separate, clean bare-metal points. Never daisy-chain grounds through the starting battery terminal — voltage drop causes charging anomalies that are frustratingly hard to diagnose in the field.
  5. Label every connection and laminate a simple circuit diagram to store in the vehicle. When something fails at a campsite at midnight, you want a reference that doesn't require memory.

Practical Knowledge

Tips for the Trail & the Mind

Field-tested camping know-how and the mindset shifts that make off-grid living stick.

⛺ Camping Tips

  • Store fuel in the shadeIn tropical heat, jerry cans left in direct sun build dangerous internal pressure. Store fuel containers in shade, leave a small amount of airspace at the top, and never fully seal a warm container. This matters most on long drives between fill-ups in the wet season.
  • Ventilate your tent when it rainsInstinct says close everything when it rains, but that's how condensation builds overnight and soaks your sleeping gear. Keep a small vent open at the top — most rain enters horizontally, not vertically — to let moisture-laden air escape rather than pool inside.
  • Boil, don't rely on filters for murky waterFilters rated for bacteria and protozoa clog quickly in high-sediment jungle streams. Pre-settle turbid water in a container for thirty minutes, decant carefully, then boil for one minute. A clean pot and fire are more reliable than a blocked filter with no backup.
  • Use a dry bag inside your packRain covers alone fail on river crossings and in monsoon downpours. Put electronics, navigation, and dry clothes inside a sealed dry bag inside your pack. It costs about $8 at any dive shop and turns a gear-destroying mistake into a non-event.
  • Hang food 2m from the trunk, not just highMost jungle animals that raid camp supplies are climbers, not jumpers. Height matters less than horizontal distance from the tree. Hang your food bag at least 2 metres out from the nearest trunk and 1.5 metres below any branch it's attached to.
  • Test your stove before you leave homeO-rings in gas valves and canister threads degrade in storage. Run your stove for five full minutes at home, check for hissing connections, and replace worn O-rings before the trip. Discovering a failed seal at the campsite, in the dark, three hours from anywhere, is completely preventable.

🌱 Self-Reliance & Mindset

  • Version 1 is always wrongEvery off-grid system you build — water, power, food, fuel — will have a version 2. The goal of version 1 is not to get it right; it's to discover which parts were wrong. Build the first version knowing this, and leave time in the budget for the rebuild.
  • Solve the closest problem firstBeginners want the complete system before they start: solar, battery, inverter, generator backup, tank, filtration. The experienced off-gridder solves the next single problem — usually water or shade — and adds complexity only after that solution proves stable.
  • Redundancy is a mindset, not a gear listEvery critical system needs a fallback: a backup lighter, a second water container, a physical map when the phone dies. The goal isn't to carry more stuff — it's to eliminate single points of failure in anything that keeps you alive or moving.
  • Failure data is more valuable than successThe gear that doesn't break tells you nothing. The system that fails in unexpected weather, the camp that floods, the filter that clogs — these are the observations that permanently upgrade your knowledge. Take a note. The failure is the curriculum.
  • Know your exit before you need itBefore you commit to a campsite or valley route, know the way out. Not because emergencies are likely — because knowing the exit costs nothing, and the mental bandwidth it frees is real. You make better decisions when you're not managing unspoken anxiety about escape.
  • Self-sufficiency is a direction, not a destinationYou will never be fully self-sufficient. The goal is to be more self-sufficient than you were last month — to have one fewer external dependency that can fail at the wrong moment. Measured that way, progress is always achievable.
