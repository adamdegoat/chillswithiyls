Gear Pick

Maxtrax MKII Recovery Boards

The difference between stuck and saved

Full-length fibre-reinforced nylon boards that give a spinning tyre something to bite on soft sand, mud, or clay. At 1,145mm long and 2.7kg each, they're light enough to reach for before the winch cable comes out. One honest limitation: they bridge two tyres at a time — if you're genuinely buried, you'll need a second pair or a systematic re-positioning sequence to work your way out.

$220–260 USD per pair (AUD ~$350–400)
Trail Intel

Mount Bromo, Tengger Semeru National Park, East Java, Indonesia

Sealed road to Cemoro Lawang village, then unsealed sand track across the Tengger caldera — minimum 200mm ground clearance required; rangers enforce it at the checkpoint.

Almost everyone joins the 3am jeep convoy to catch sunrise from the main viewpoint, which means thousands of vehicles descending simultaneously. Arrive at the caldera rim by 6:30am and you'll have the sand sea nearly to yourself with warmer light. The floor route between Bromo's crater and the Widodaren viewpoint is the stretch most people skip entirely — and it's the best part of the whole drive.

Best season: April–October (dry season — the caldera floor track becomes a sand trap after wet-season rains)
Build Tip

Wiring a DC-DC charger to your auxiliary LiFePO4 battery

  1. Select a DC-DC charger matched to your alternator output — the Victron Orion-Tr Smart 30A or REDARC BCDC1225D are well-proven options. For a 100Ah LiFePO4 bank, 25–30A input is the right range; pushing more loads the alternator hard at idle.
  2. Run a cable (minimum 6 B&S / 10 AWG) from your starter battery positive terminal through a 50A ANL fuse mounted as close to the starter battery as possible. This fuse protects the wiring run — it needs to be at the source, not mid-run.
  3. Mount the charger in a ventilated spot away from heat sources. Most DC-DC chargers operate to 40°C and throttle output above that — engine bay mounting works but keep it away from the exhaust manifold and the firewall hot zone.
  4. Run the output cable to the auxiliary battery positive through a second ANL fuse, and run dedicated earth cables from both the charger body and the aux battery negative to a clean chassis ground — not daisy-chained together.
  5. Set the charge profile for LiFePO4: 14.2–14.6V absorption, 13.6V float. Most modern DC-DC chargers have a preset for this, but verify the voltage setpoints in the configuration menu before connecting the battery.

Practical Knowledge

Tips for the Trail & the Mind

Field-tested camping know-how and the mindset shifts that make off-grid living stick.

⛺ Camping Tips

  • Pack for 10° colder at nightEven in summer, temperatures drop fast after dark. Bring at least one extra layer — a cheap fleece takes up almost no space and makes the difference between a great trip and a miserable one.
  • Arrive before sunsetSetting up camp in the dark is the fastest way to lose gear, misread terrain, and ruin your first night. Give yourself two hours of light minimum to pick a spot, pitch properly, and get your fire going before you need it.
  • The three-point water ruleAlways carry three water sources: a main supply, a backup filter, and a backup to the backup (purification tablets). Running dry in the field is a survival situation, not an inconvenience.
  • Keep your fire small and manageableA fire the size of a dinner plate throws enough heat to cook, warm yourself, and keep spirits up — without being a liability. Big fires burn through wood fast, are harder to control, and leave a scorched mess behind.
  • Leave your campsite better than you found itPack out every piece of rubbish, scatter your ash, and restore ground cover where possible. The access to these places depends on the people who visit them acting like they care — because they should.
  • Tell someone where you're goingBefore any remote trip, share your route, campsite, and expected return time with someone at home. No app, satellite communicator, or vehicle setup replaces this single habit.

🌱 Self-Reliance & Mindset

  • Discomfort is the lessonThe cold morning, the failed fire, the wrong turn — these aren't problems with the trip. They are the trip. The ability to stay calm when things go sideways is built outside, not in a classroom.
  • Reduce before you optimizeThe most capable off-grid setups are almost always the simplest ones. Before buying a new piece of gear, ask what you can remove. Less to carry, less to break, less to depend on.
  • Do one thing without your phoneRead the map. Start the fire. Cook the meal. Pick one task per day in the field and do it analog. The goal isn't to perform simplicity — it's to rebuild the confidence that you can operate without a screen.
  • Small wins compound out hereA fire started on the first strike. Water filtered and ready before you're thirsty. Camp set before dark. None of these feel like much alone — but they stack, and after a few days you carry yourself differently.
  • Nature resets your baselineThree days away from artificial light, constant noise, and scheduled obligations genuinely shifts what feels like "normal." You don't need to go forever — you just need to go long enough to remember what quiet actually feels like.
  • Self-sufficiency is a practice, not a destinationYou never fully arrive. Every trip reveals a gap — a skill you don't have, a dependency you didn't notice. That gap is the point. The practice is showing up to close it, one trip at a time.
